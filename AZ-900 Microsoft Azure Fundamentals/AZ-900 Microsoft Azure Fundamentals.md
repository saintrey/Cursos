Storage Redundancy
When it comes to storing critical data, you need to know that your data is
protected against unexpected failures. This is where the concept of storage
redundancy can help put your mind at ease. When it comes to working with
technology, unfortunately, accidents do occasionally happen. These could be in
the form of hardware failures, such as a corrupted disk, or accidents can have a
wider scope in which a whole location, such as an availability zone or a region, is
temporarily unavailable. For these type of scenarios, the concept of data
redundancy can help protect against these unplanned accidents and
failures. From a broad perspective, how data redundancy works is that it creates
multiple replicated copies of your data, typically copying your data across
multiple disks. When properly configured, if one copy of that disk fails, that data is
still readily available because that data is still available on the other replicated
disks. From an Azure perspective, Azure Storage will always create multiple
copies of your data. This replication process is completely automatic. At
minimum, you will always have at least 3 copies of your data, depending on your
redundancy options, and the process is entirely invisible to the end user. From
your perspective, you're only interacting with a single copy of your data in Azure
Storage, but behind the scenes, there are actually multiple copies being
replicated to multiple locations. What you do have control or interaction
with, however, is the different redundancy options that you can choose. In Azure,
there are 4 redundancy options available which have different scopes, such as
different locations, in which you could have redundancy within a single
zone, multiple zones in a single region, and it can be replicated across multiple
regions as well. These options are going to be the primary focus of this
lesson. Keep in mind going into it, though, is that the higher availability
redundancy options do come with a bit of a higher cost as a trade off for that
higher availability. So with that mind, let's take a look at the 4 different
redundancy options available within Azure Storage, which is, again, going to be a
main exam point we need to be familiar with. These redundancy options are
roughly broken up into 2 main categories in which you have redundancy options
in a single region and redundancy options within multiple regions, specifically to
paired regions. Within our single region category, we have locally redundant
storage or LRS, zone-redundant storage or ZRS, and within the multi-region
category, we have geo-redundant storage GRS, and geo-zone redundant storage
or GZRS for short. Regardless of which redundancy option we choose, all options
include, at a minimum, 3 copies of your data within our primary region, and for our
multi-region options, you will also have 3 copies in the secondary region as
well. So, in other words, you will have either 3 or 6 copies of your data being
replicated, depending on if we go with one of the single or multi-region
redundancy scopes. Let's go ahead and break each of these options in a bit more
detail, starting with our lowest tier option, which is locally redundant storage, or
LRS for short. LRS is our lowest cost redundancy option because it creates
3 copies of your data within a single location or, in other words, within a single
data center or a single availability zone. As I mentioned, this is our lowest cost
option if cost is a factor, and it is still useful in which it will protect against a single
disk failure, specifically these copies that are located on different storage racks in
your single data center. So even if an entire server rack becomes unavailable, you
still have 2 other server racks safely storing your data. However, since all of our
copies are in a single data center or a single zone, this option does not protect
against temporary zone or region-wide outages. If this availability zone is
unavailable, our data is also unavailable because all copies of our data are within
that single availability zone. Well, what do we do then if we want to have a little
bit more redundancy or high-availability in case of a single zone failure? In which
case we would go up to our next redundancy option, which is zone redundant
storage, or ZRS for short. Zone redundant storage is also hosted in a single
region. However, those 3 copies of your data, instead of being hosted in a single
location or availability zone, it instead spans 3 different availability zones. In other
words, we will have one copy of our data in each availability zone within that
region. This increases our reliability or high-availability in that it will protect
against a zone outage, in which if AZ1 is unavailable, our data is still accessible
because AZ2 and AZ3 also hosts our data. However, it does not protect against a
region-wide outage because all of our data is replicated within a single zone. So
what do we do then if we want to protect against region-wide outages? Well, we
would go up to our next redundancy option known as geo-redundant storage, or
GRS for short. With geo-redundant storage, we have 3 copies in 2 different
regions. 3 copies will be in our primary region physical location in an LRS or a
single zone configuration, and we have an additional 3 copies, or 6 total, in our
secondary or paired region, also in a single LRS configuration within a single data
center or availability zone. This increases our reliability in that it helps
protect against a primary region failure. In other words, if Region 1 becomes
unavailable, Azure Storage will then failover to Region 2. However, it does not
give us zone redundancy in our primary region because, again, we are working
with an LRS single zone redundancy option in that primary region. One additional
feature to be aware of that is unique to our multi-region options is that you also
have the optional ability to configure read access from your secondary region for
high-availability scenarios. This option is enabled by default. You have to
explicitly choose to disable this option. However, how it works is that in the event
of a primary region failure, the failover process of accessing our data from our
secondary region will be relatively much quicker than if we had this option
disabled. So, at this point, what if we want to have the maximum amount of
redundancy possible, even if it does come at a higher cost in which we
want multi-zone redundancy within our primary region and also redundancy
within a paired region as well? Well, in that case, we would go to our highest tier
redundancy option of geo-zone-redundancy storage, or GZRS for short. This is,
again, the maximum redundancy option. With GZRS we will, again, have 3 copies
of our data within our primary region. However, it will be in a zone with an option
in which each copy is spanned across 3 different availability zones, and we will
also have 3 copies in our secondary region as well. However, that is still in an LRS
or single zone configuration. This option gives us maximum redundancy in which
it can protect against both a primary region failure, which would, again, failover to
our secondary region, and we can also tolerate a zone failure within our individual
region without having to then failover to our secondary region as well. And like
our other multi-region option, we, again, have the ability to optionally configure
read access from our secondary region for high-availability or, in other words, to
give us fast failover capability. So, wow, we covered quite a bit of ground when it
comes to the multiple redundancy options available on Azure. Let's go in and
quickly sum things up before we move on. From a high level, we learned how
Azure Storage automatically and completely invisible to you replicates or creates
copies of your data for redundancy. These will always be 3 copies in our single
region, and if we have a paired region, there will be 6 copies total, in which you
have 3 in our primary region and 3 in our secondary region. The 4 different
redundancy options available on Azure Storage vary by availability in cost in
which it is scoped to either a single zone in your primary region, multiple zones
within your single region, and across regions we can choose to copy data across
either a single zone or multiple zones within our primary region. Once again, there
is an inverse relationship between the availability of your data, depending on a
zone or a region failure, and the cost we have to pay for those redundancy

Before we dive further into authentication topics with Azure Active Directory, we

sensitive resources. By comparison then, users and devices outside of that
corporate network are considered to be non-trusted devices. So users,
computers, and databases inside of our corporate network or our trusted
perimeter are implicitly trusted, and anyone outside of that network is not
trusted. So this model's worked relatively well over the years, however, in the
newer modern workplace, there are some challenges with this trusted perimeter
model. The biggest challenge is, again, since we have a perimeter model, you
must be within that trusted perimeter or that corporate network in order to
access our sensitive corporate resources. Now surprisingly, this makes remote
work, in other words, working outside the office, a bit of a challenge because they
are not inside of that trusted perimeter. Now, the typical workaround for this

VPN client on those mobile devices or other workarounds. Additionally, if we

Public and Private Endpoints
Let's continue our discussions on securing network access to Azure by now

services, specifically our Platform as a Service services by discussing public and
private endpoints. So when we are discussing public and private endpoints what
we are referring to specifically is our managed services or our Azure Platform as a
Service services. What we're talking about is that by default when you create
these public services, such as Azure Storage, Azure SQL, and others, these
services are publicly reachable or publicly exposed over the public
internet. Now, there are a couple of different aspects with this we need to be
familiar with. First of all, when accessing these public services over a virtual
network, specifically a virtual machine inside of your virtual network, traffic from
that virtual network to your managed service, in this case Azure Storage, actually
traverses the public internet. Additionally, these managed services end
points, which are again by default public endpoints, are also exposed to the
general public. Now, keep in mind, this does not mean that the contents of
those resources are accessible to the public. We still need proper authentication
to grant access. However, these resources are still publicly exposed or publicly
reachable, which in scenarios in which your services contain sensitive
content, this could be a little bit of a problem. So what then are we to do if we
want to limit or remove that public exposure from our different Azure managed
services? Well, fortunately we have 2 different native services to choose
from, which I call a "good" and a "better" solution. The "good" solution that we are
going to refer to is known as service endpoints, and the "better" solution, which
from the lesson title is going to be our primary focus, is going to be that of private
endpoints. So again, service endpoints is our good solution and private endpoints
is our better solution. Let's unpack those in a bit more detail starting with service
endpoints, again our good solution. Not the best, but it is still good. How service
endpoints work is that allows you to privately connect a virtual network subnet to
different Azure Platform as a Service services. Specifically, it will enable a direct
connection from that individual subnet to that managed service. What this means
specifically is that resources inside of that subnet, like virtual machines, will
connect over Microsoft's private backbone from that subnet to that managed
service, as opposed to traversing the public internet to get to that managed
service instead. With service endpoints enabled you also have the optional yet
highly recommended ability to also configure the managed service itself, in this
example, Azure Storage, to only allow traffic from that service endpoint enabled
subnet to that service, therefore cutting access off to everywhere else. And you
also have the ability for on-premises or non-Azure locations to also optionally
restrict access to specific public IP addresses. So in a scenario in which we have
a home office which also needs to securely access this managed service, we have
the ability to limit public exposure from specific public IP addresses only, which
we can also combine with service endpoint access from our VNet subnets, as
well. Overall, this greatly limits the exposure of our managed service from the
public internet. However, I did mention that service endpoints are only a good
solution, therefore there are some limitations of service endpoints that you'd
need to be familiar with. The first of which, from a private networking
perspective, this provides secure access to only Azure virtual networks. There is
no ability within service endpoints to provide private access not over the public
internet to our different non-Azure or on-premises locations. As we mentioned in
the previous slide, our only other option is to only allow on-premise access from
public IP addresses. However, even though this is an improvement, it is still going
over the public internet even though we are restricting access to a small portion
of public IPs only. With that in mind, our managed service still has a public
endpoint that still exists. In other words, that connection is still not truly
private, and there is still some public internet exposure. And then our last
limitation of service endpoints is that service endpoints provide private access to
the entirety of a managed service, not a specific instance of a managed
service. For example, when we set up service endpoints with a VNet subnet, it
provides private access to the entirety of the Azure Storage service, not just to a
single individual storage account. So the scope could be a little bit too wide for
some people's comfort. So, with these limitations in mind of our good
solution, again it's not a bad solution, but it could be better. Let's now talk about
private endpoints, which is the better solution which resolve these limitations
of our previous good solution of service endpoints. How private endpoints works
as opposed to service endpoints that it acts as a managed network interface that
sits inside of your virtual network subnet. This managed network interface will
provide a private connection to a specific instance of the service. In other
words, it provides private access to only a single storage account or only a single
Azure SQLs instance and others, as well. Another advantage of private endpoints
as opposed to service endpoints is that it provides truly private connectivity over
other connected networks, such as hybrid or on-premises network connecting to
our Azure virtual network over a VPN or express route connection, and it also
provides add-on private connectivity to other virtual networks paired with our
original Azure virtual network. In other words, that Azure virtual network can
share its private connectivity over a private endpoint with other networks
connected to it, as well, whether it is an on-premises connection, VPN
connection or a peered connection, as well. And then our final advantage of
private endpoints as opposed to service endpoints is that you have the ability of
completely disabling the public access or exposure to our connected service. In
other words, a private endpoint allows you to make that managed service
truly private with no public exposure whatsoever. In other words, that public
endpoint is completely disabled as opposed to only limiting traffic from certain
public IP ranges. So again, there are a number of reasons why private endpoints
are the better private managed service connectivity solution compared to
service endpoints. Finishing things up, let's go over a quick scenario that would
